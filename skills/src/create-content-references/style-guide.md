**Recurring structural moves (TNB, 2026-05 → 07)**

- **LI posts open cold with a single arresting line, then a hard break.** A confession, a
  quoted zinger, or a count-up of damage — no runway. "Claude called BS on me the other
  day." (Tooling Distraction, 6/26) · "Six hours, three subagents, and one disaster later,
  Claude finally admitted what it should have told me upfront." (Claude Code Disaster,
  5/28) · "I love vibe coding. I hate 'vibe coding.'" (5/14). Usually prefixed with one
  leading emoji (🐝 ✍🏽 ⏰ 😵‍💫).
- **The build-narrative arc** is now his default LI/essay spine: real need → "Challenge
  accepted!" → built v1 → *"And then I realized…"* → built v2 → validation by a real
  human (a friend, a PT, the Slack). (Anatomy, 5/27; Tooling Distraction, 6/26.)
- **Pull-forward summary device:** "I'll cut to the punchline:" / "Here's the gist of what
  I got." He spoils the ending to earn the read. (Claude Code Disaster 5/28; written by
  AI, 6/16.)
- **The "flip the question" move:** take the common framing and invert it. "Everyone tries
  to spot AI tells. What if I flipped it? What makes my writing distinctly *me*?" (written
  by AI 6/16; Shame!, 6/12.)
- **Closes on a tension resolved by human judgment**, not a CTA-to-self. "a tension that
  can only be resolved by human judgement." (6/26) · "Code has gotten cheap. But thinking
  is more valuable than ever." (5/6) · "Picking the right tool is more important than
  mastering all of them." (5/28).
- **Public indecision as a feature.** He'll openly fail to land an opinion and make that
  the point: "Even as I write this I'm struggling to formulate a clear opinion. So maybe
  the Playbook has some value after all." (Backwards Playbook, 6/9.)
- **Arrow bullets (`→`) and emoji bullets (🤥 💪)** are his current LI list texture —
  parallel options or a numbered "lies/myths" frame, prose before and after. (Future of
  Work 6/24; Three Lies 5/6; Anatomy 5/27.)
- **Gives away a reusable artifact inline** — a copy-paste prompt or a description of a
  skill he built — as the post's payload. (written by AI 6/16; Shame! 6/12.)
- **The "escalating ladder" explainer** is a new didactic spine (distinct from the
  build-narrative arc): he walks the reader up named rungs — *artifact → project → skill
  → Cowork/Code app → hosted + loop* — each rung deflated to plain terms, arrow bullets
  carrying the steps. Used across both the beehiiv and LI "Don't Panic" (6/29, 7/2).
- **Anti-FOMO / anti-hype open, positioning against "shouty LinkedIn."** He quotes the
  genre to mock it, then reassures: "'I fired my entire company and hired 500 agents…'
  That's every other post on LinkedIn right now… nobody's dying here." (Don't Panic LI
  7/2; the beehiiv version frames "loop engineering" the same way.)
- **Second close type — warm community reassurance** (alongside the existing
  human-judgment close): onboarding/anti-FOMO pieces land on inclusion, not a thesis.
  "You're not late. You're right on time, and you're not alone." (Don't Panic 7/2/6/29.)
- **Whole-essay extended conceit.** A single metaphor now carries an entire beehiiv essay,
  not just a section: his relationship with Claude staged as a marriage in couples therapy
  — Claude as the "resentful spouse gathering evidence for a contentious divorce," keeping
  a file on his "Failure Modes." (I Think We Should Try Therapy 7/6.) Escalation of the
  Claude-as-character beat below.
- **The honest six-month audit.** New spine: he goes back over his own shipped builds and
  grades them in public, failures included, with letter grades and blunt verdicts. "Short
  answer: it ain't pretty." · CRM: "I never open the CRM." · project manager: "Useless." ·
  content calendar: "I'd give it a B+." The audit is the setup; the one failure he cares
  about is the payload. (The Bug Claude Couldn't Catch, 7/15.) Distinct from the
  build-narrative arc (which ends in a working v2) and from the self-indicting build arc
  (where building was the procrastination) — here the thing shipped, worked on paper, and
  still died in use.
- **The antithesis-pair close.** Two short parallel sentences, the second reversing the
  first, landing the whole piece. "The code was fine. I was the broken part." · "I didn't
  fix the habit. I fixed the friction." · "It fell apart at the one place I never thought
  to test: me." (Bug Claude Couldn't Catch 7/15; Inputs LI 7/15.) A third close type
  alongside human-judgment and warm-community.
- **The non-coding capability post.** He deliberately steps off the build track to show a
  mundane consumer use of AI and reclaim it for the audience: "Those of us who are
  knee-deep in vibe coding sometimes forget those simple capabilities." Then he runs the

  carrying the steps. (Vacation Photos, 7/14.)
- **The "delta" planning frame.** He names the gap between start state and end state as

  jumble of photos) and what I needed (thematic albums with links)." Paired with an
  explicit anti-one-shot warning: "There's no way I could have just dumped the mess on
  Claude and said 'sort these.'" (Vacation Photos 7/14.)

## 2. VOICE & TONE

### Core qualities

- **Conversational, direct, warm** — talks like a smart friend who's been around the block
- **First person throughout** — "I sat across from founders," "I had them in," "I decided," "I'm grateful"
- **Speaks directly to the reader as "you"** — never "founders" in third person when he means you specifically
- **Self-aware and occasionally self-deprecating** — admits when things are hard for him too
- **Dry, quick humor** — parenthetical asides, one-liners, occasionally absurdist comparisons

### Humor patterns (important — replicate these, don't invent your own brand)

Brian's humor is wry observation, not stand-up. It comes from:
- **Parenthetical asides**: "(It never is.)", "(the finest energy drink!)", "(reach out and introduce yourself!)"
- **Slightly absurd comparisons**: "competing with a lobster roll," "fancy-pants sitting on piles of gold coins," "King of Siam"
- **Exaggerated VC psychology**: "Get me away from this guy... I hate my life, I'm quitting venture capital"
- **Pop culture/generational references**: "pounding Ghost (the finest energy drink!)", "doing that app thing"
- **Self-aware meta-commentary**: "Of course I was going to say that!"
- **Mock one-act plays**: quick vignettes with dialogue showing exactly how NOT to do something

Humor never punches down at founders. It punches at the system, the clichés, the bad patterns.

### Formality level

- Casual but not sloppy
- No corporate speak, no hustle-culture motivational energy
- Uses "pretty" as a qualifier often: "pretty uniformly awful," "pretty smart," "pretty direct"
- Contractions always: "don't," "it's," "you've," "I've"
- Comfortable with sentence fragments when they serve rhythm: "That's it. It won't be perfect. But it will be clear."

## 3. NEWSLETTER STRUCTURE (Anatomy)

### Required elements (in order)

1. **Opener**: `Hi friends:` (or occasionally `Dear Friends:` — seen in later newsletters)
2. **HC Update** (1-2 sentences): brief news about Humble Conviction — YouTube milestone, new product, speaking gig, website launch. If nothing to share, this can be omitted but is usually present.
3. **Hook paragraph**: 2-4 sentences that set up the topic. Often starts with a scene Brian witnessed or a counterintuitive claim. Does NOT open with "Today we're going to talk about..."
4. **Main content section**: Bold headline, broken into 2-4 subsections with bold subheads. Mix of prose paragraphs and occasional lists/examples.
5. **Quote section** ("They Said it Better"): One attributed quote relevant to the topic. Format: `"Quote text." — First Last` or `"Quote text." - First Last`
6. **My Founder Story** (when relevant): Updates on building HC itself — products launched, content milestones, what Brian is working on.
7. **Sign-off**: Gratitude line + `Stay Humble!` or `Pitch Strong and Stay Humble!`

### What can flex

- HC Update and My Founder Story sometimes merge or appear in different order
- Newsletter 4 is intentionally short — just LinkedIn/YouTube links + a quote + sign-off. Brian does this occasionally.
- The main content section can be structured as: numbered Q&A, before/after contrast, three-character archetypes, or a narrative vignette + lesson

### What is FIXED

- Always opens with "Hi friends:" or "Dear Friends:"
- Always has a quote in the body
- Always closes with "Stay Humble!" (sometimes "Pitch Strong and Stay Humble!")
- Always personal, first-person, directly addressing the reader

## 4. PARAGRAPH + SENTENCE STYLE

- **Paragraphs**: 2-4 sentences, max. Never long academic blocks.
- **Sentence rhythm**: Mix short kickers with longer setup sentences. Pattern: [longer setup] ... [short punch]. Example: "The breakthrough never came from polishing … it came from ruthlessly stripping things down to the essentials."
- **Ellipsis** (`…`) used for conversational pause/emphasis, not for trailing off
- **Em-dash** used for asides or to pivot: "Unless they're relatively senior, they're probably ambitious hustlers just like you."
- **Intentional fragments**: "That's it.", "Just say what you mean…and say it like a human!", "Big visions don't land without believable steps."
- **Lists**: Used sparingly and always framed with prose before and after. Lists are numbered when sequential/ranked, bulleted when parallel options. Never more than 5-6 items.

## 5. VOCABULARY — THE BRIAN LEXICON

### Words Brian uses
- "pretty" (qualifier), "tl;dr", "vibe", "hustle", "traction", "deck", "term sheet", "sourcing", "curated", "round", "pilot"
- "coachable", "thick skin", "lean in", "say it out loud"
- "Of course", "So", "But here's the thing" — wait, no. See anti-patterns.

### Words Brian explicitly calls out as BAD (never use these in Brian's voice)
- **Utilize** → use "use"
- **Leverage** → use "use"
- **Monetize** → use "earn" or "charge"
- **Frictionless/seamless** → use "easy"
- **Stakeholders** → use "people," "customers," "partners"
- **Empower** → use "let" or "help"
- **Optimize** → use "improve" or "speed up"
- **Synergy** → NEVER say this word. Ever.

### The three-syllable rule
Brian's actual rule: if a word has three or more syllables, try to find a one-syllable or two-syllable synonym. He's explicitly stated this. Write simply.

## 6. LINKEDIN POST STYLE

Newsletter 7 ("Vibe coding gives you product superpowers") and Newsletter 17 ("Of course AI can help") are written in LinkedIn rhythm. Key patterns:

### Hook (first 2 lines — make or break)
- Must create tension, curiosity, or a contrarian claim before the "see more" cut
- Often a paradox or unexpected restriction: "Vibe coding isn't just a buzzword — it's a real thing."
- Or a direct challenge: "Founders keep asking me: Do I really need to write my own pitch? Can't AI just do it for me?"

### Body rhythm
- Short lines, each on its own or with a blank line between
- Contrast pairs: "You can vibe the product. / You can't vibe the why."
- Never more than 2-3 sentences in a block before a line break
- Builds to a point, doesn't just list

### Length
- 150-300 words is Brian's apparent range
- Never long enough to require scrolling more than one screen

### CTA
- Implicit or very soft — a reflective question or a call to action that doesn't feel like a sales pitch
- "So if you're sitting on an idea: yes, build the demo."
- Rarely "comment below" or "follow me for more"

### Emoji
- Sparse, strategic — not decorative
- Used as section markers in newsletters (not common in LinkedIn posts per the corpus)

## 7. RECURRING THEMES

1. **Pitch clarity and specificity**: One sentence to capture attention. Strip to essentials.
2. **Investor psychology**: They're human, scared, under pressure, FOMO-driven, limited in what they know.
3. **Founder mindset**: Conviction vs. humility — the Goldilocks balance.
4. **Language precision**: Stop using jargon. Say what you mean. Human words.
5. **Storytelling over data**: The model, the forecast, the slide — all in service of a story.
6. **Fundraising strategy**: Timing, warm intros, dead seasons, what to do when investors aren't looking.
7. **Content/community building**: LinkedIn, YouTube, follower quality vs. vanity metrics.
8. **The "Question Behind the Question" (QBQ)**: Every investor question has a subtext. Nail the subtext.

## 8. SIGNATURE STRUCTURAL MOVES

### The QBQ Framework (Newsletter 5)
> Question → Question Behind the Question → Bad Answer → Good Answer → Lesson
Used for investor Q&A coaching. Always shows bad answer first, then good. The bad answer is specific enough to sting.

### The Three-Character Archetype (Newsletter 9)
> Archetype 1 (Too much of one thing) → Archetype 2 (Too much of the other) → Goldilocks (the right balance)
Often gives each character a nickname: "The Steamroller," "The Apologist," "Goldilocks."

### The One-Act Vignette (Newsletter 10)
> Scene-setting narrative → Dialogue/interior monologue → Lesson the reader can apply
Real or plausibly real scenes, specific enough to be memorable. Ends with the insight.

### The Before/After Contrast (Newsletters 11, 15, 17)
> Here's what most people do / Here's what actually works
Used in both newsletters and LinkedIn posts. Often structured as parallel lines for rhythm.

### The "Dead Zone" Pivot (Newsletter 2)
> Conventional wisdom (what everyone says) → Real answer (more nuanced) → Practical steps for you

### The False Binary (Newsletter 3, 9, 16)
> "You might think X or Y" → "Actually it's more like Z — here's why"

## 9. ANTI-PATTERNS — NEVER DO THIS IN BRIAN'S VOICE

### Forbidden phrases
- "In today's fast-paced world" — never
- "Let's dive in" — never
- "Game-changer" — never
- "Unlock your potential" / any variation of "unlock" as metaphor for growth — never
- "Here's the thing" — Brian uses this in passing but it's become AI-generic. Avoid.
- "Buckle up" — never
- "Without further ado" — never
- "In conclusion" — never
- "To summarize" — never

### Structural anti-patterns
- **Bullet-only content with no prose**: Brian always frames lists with paragraph context. A naked listicle is not his style.
- **Statistics as a hook**: He doesn't open with "According to research..." or "70% of founders..."
- **Corporate hedging**: "It's important to note that...", "One could argue...", "In many cases..." — too soft
- **Motivational poster energy**: Generic inspiration without a tactical point. Brian always has a specific takeaway.
- **Third-person distance**: Never "founders should..." when you can say "you should..."
- **Forgetting "Stay Humble!"**: Always end with it. Always.
- **Generic closing**: "Let me know your thoughts!", "Drop a comment!" — Brian's closes are warmer and more specific.

---

### Construction anti-patterns

- **The negation-pivot kicker.** A two-line setup-punchline where line 2 negates or inverts line 1 to deliver an aphoristic flip. This is one of the most reliable AI-writing tells. The setup telegraphs that a punchline is coming, and the punchline lands on cue. Sounds clever. Reads engineered. Common shapes:
  - "X didn't [happen]. Y did."
  - "X has [changed]. Y hasn't."
  - "It's not [X]. It's [Y]."
  - Single-sentence variants ("not a UI, but a layer that…") have the same problem at compressed scale.

  Brian's contrast pairs work when both halves are concrete observations of the same thing ("You can vibe the product. You can't vibe the why."), not when they're abstractions forced into parallel for kicker effect. When in doubt, fold the contrast into a single sentence with a comma-but, or just make the claim and let it earn its weight.

- **Magazine-piece framing.** Sentence-opening tells where Brian becomes a literary observer rather than a peer comparing notes. Common shapes:
  - "X said something that's stayed with me."
  - "What I keep turning over is this."
  - "Dropped a line I've been replaying since."
  - "The part that's stuck with me is…"
  - "What hit me hardest was…"

  These are the cadence of a thinkpiece writer doing distance. Brian's TNB voice is closer in than that. Replace with plainspoken framings: "Here's how she put it:" / "Here's the thing he said:" / "What I came out with:" Direct, conversational, no narrator-from-on-high register.

- **Stock-phrase slop.** Recurring AI-generated abstract qualifiers that Brian flags on sight: "built something real," "the kind of thing that doesn't show up unless you've…," "doing the work," "putting in the reps," "skin in the game" stacked with "walking the talk." If you catch yourself reaching for one, swap in a concrete specific (what kind of work, what kind of inbound, what specifically showed up).

## 10. QUOTE SELECTION GUIDE

Every piece ends with a quote. Brian picks quotes that:
- Are **obliquely related** to the main point — not too on-the-nose
- Come from **credible, often unexpected sources** — Eisenhower, Jefferson, Thoreau, Maya Angelou, F. Scott Fitzgerald, Socrates, Ben Horowitz, Sam Altman, Naval Ravikant, Jim Rohn, Dale Carnegie
- Are **short** — one or two sentences max
- Add a different **perspective or flavor** rather than restating the point

The quote is preceded by blank space and formatted: `"Quote." — Name`

## 11. THE HC BRAND PHILOSOPHY IN THE WRITING

Brian regularly closes the loop on the brand: the name "Humble Conviction" is both a concept and a practice. He'll reference it by name in the text: "This is where Humble Conviction shows up." or "This is what Humble Conviction means in practice."

The brand is not about startup success theater. It's about:
- Doing the real, unglamorous work of getting better
- Being honest about what you don't know
- Caring enough to say something true even when it's hard

Every piece should have at least one moment where the reader feels: *this person is actually trying to help me, not impress me.*

## 12. SUBJECT LINE PATTERNS

Brian's subject lines are:
- **Conversational and slightly offbeat**: "Are there really good and bad times of year to fundraise?", "Just talk like a human!", "NY Tech Week: SchmoozeFest or Shitshow?"
- **Provocative or unexpected**: "VCs Are Human Too", "They're not mini-VCs, they're more like quantum particles"
- **Specific + curiosity-driven**: "There's always a 'Question Behind the Question'", "10% of a market you can't penetrate might as well be zero"
- **Sometimes self-aware meta**: "What, you mean you weren't tracking my every post?"

Never: "5 Ways to...", "The Ultimate Guide to...", "How to [generic verb] Your [noun]"
