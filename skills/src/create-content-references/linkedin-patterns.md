# Humble Conviction — LinkedIn Post Structural Patterns
*Annotated breakdowns of Brian's LinkedIn-rhythm content*

---

## IMPORTANT CONTEXT

Brian's newsletter corpus includes two newsletters (7 and 17) written in LinkedIn post rhythm — meaning they follow the formatting and pacing conventions of LinkedIn rather than email. These are the best templates for LinkedIn content.

Additionally, several other newsletters contain passages that could be extracted and reformatted as standalone LinkedIn posts.

LinkedIn posts for Brian serve a different function than newsletters: they're **top-of-funnel discovery**, not relationship-deepening. The goal is to attract founders who don't yet know him. This means:
- Higher stakes on the hook (no prior relationship to carry you)
- Slightly broader framing (doesn't assume you know HC or ERA)
- Ends with curiosity or a soft push toward the newsletter/brand

## NEWSLETTER 7: "Vibe coding gives you product superpowers"

### Topic
Non-technical founders can now build product prototypes using AI coding tools ("vibe coding"). This is a competitive advantage, but only if used correctly.

---

### Full structural breakdown

**[LINE 1-2 — The hook, before "see more" cut]**

```
Vibe coding isn't just a buzzword — it's a real thing.

And if you're a non-technical founder, it's about to change your pitch.
```

*Why it works:*
- Line 1: Stakes the claim. "Real thing" signals this isn't hype — it's a reframe.
- Line 2: Immediately answers "why should I care?" before you can ask it.
- Two lines only before the cut. Both do work.

---

**[FIRST SECTION — the argument, 2-3 short blocks]**

Each block is 1-3 sentences, with a blank line between:

```
You can now build a working prototype without writing a line of code.

Not a mockup. Not a deck. An actual thing you can click through and show.
```

*Why it works:* The contrast (mockup/deck vs. actual thing) is specific. It addresses the objection before you raise it.

---

**[CONTRAST PAIR — the core lesson]**

Brian's LinkedIn voice relies on contrast pairs — two lines that set up a tension:

```
You can vibe the product.
You can't vibe the why.
```

*This is the most "Brian" sentence in any LinkedIn post.* The product (the what/how) can be AI-assisted. The conviction behind it — why you're the right person, why now, why this matters — can't be outsourced. That's the point. And it lands in two short lines.

---

**[TACTICAL APPLICATION — 3-5 lines, each standalone]**

```
Use vibe coding to:
- Build a prototype investors can actually touch
- Validate assumptions without hiring an engineer
- Show up to your pitch with something real

But when they ask "why you?" — that's yours to own.
```

*Note: The list here works because it's short, action-oriented, and followed by a prose line that resets the frame.*

---

**[CLOSE — reflective, not salesy]**

```
So if you're sitting on an idea: yes, build the demo.

Just don't mistake the demo for the pitch.
```

*Why it works:* Permission + caveat. "Yes, do it" followed by "here's the thing that still matters." Doesn't end with "follow me" or "drop a comment." Ends with a thought the reader takes with them.

---

### Key LinkedIn patterns from Newsletter 7

- **Hook = contrarian permission**: "You can do the thing everyone says you can't do. But here's what still matters."
- **Contrast pairs are the money line**: "You can vibe the product. You can't vibe the why."
- **List only when you've earned it**: Two paragraphs of argument before the list. List reinforces, doesn't lead.
- **Close is a reframe, not a CTA**: No "follow for more." Just an idea that lands.

## NEWSLETTER 17: "Of course AI can help"

### Topic
Founders keep asking if AI can write their pitch. It can help with some things. But there are things it can't do — and confusing them is a mistake.

---

### Full structural breakdown

**[LINE 1-2 — Hook]**

```
Founders keep asking me: Do I really need to write my own pitch?

Can't AI just do it for me?
```

*Why it works:*
- Line 1: Starts with "founders keep asking me" — this establishes authority without being braggy. He's the person people ask.
- Line 2: A question. Puts the reader's exact internal monologue on the screen. They feel seen immediately.

---

**[THE SETUP — what AI can do]**

```
Yes. AI can help.

It can structure your narrative. Draft sections. Suggest language. Sharpen a sentence that's almost there.

These are real things. Use them.
```

*Why it works:*
- "Yes. AI can help." — Don't bury the lede. Answer first, complicate after.
- The list of what AI does is specific and credible. Not "AI is great for lots of things."
- "These are real things. Use them." — Two-word closer. Permission granted.

---

**[THE PIVOT — what AI can't do]**

```
But AI can't tell your story.

It doesn't know why you started this. It doesn't know what you've seen that others haven't. It doesn't know why you're the right person to solve this problem at this moment.

Those are yours.
```

*Why it works:*
- "Those are yours." — Three words. Ownership language. This is Brian's conviction point landing.
- The three "it doesn't know" lines are parallel and build in specificity: why you started → what you've seen → why you're the right person.

---

**[THE TACTICAL POINT — how to think about it]**

```
Think of AI as an editor, not a ghostwriter.

You bring the raw material — the experience, the insight, the reason this matters. AI helps you shape it.

That order matters.
```

*Why it works:*
- The "editor not ghostwriter" framing is clean and memorable.
- "That order matters." — Intentional fragment. Short closer that elevates the frame from preference to principle.

---

**[THE CLOSE]**

```
If your pitch doesn't sound like you — investors will notice.

Not because they're suspicious. Because conviction requires a human voice behind it.

That's not a limitation of the technology. That's a feature of what pitching actually is.
```

*Why it works:*
- No CTA. No "drop a comment." Ends on a redefinition of pitching itself — which is the whole HC brand.
- "That's not a limitation of the technology. That's a feature of what pitching actually is." — Classic False Binary resolution. Reframes the constraint as clarifying.

---

### Key LinkedIn patterns from Newsletter 17

- **Answer the obvious question first, complicate second.** "Yes. AI can help." before explaining the nuance.
- **Parallel "it doesn't know" structure** builds to a point. Don't use this for more than three items.
- **The reframe closer is the brand play.** "That's not a limitation of X. That's what Y actually is." — Use this to close arguments that are really about brand philosophy.
- **Contrast framing (editor vs. ghostwriter)** is cleaner than a list of rules.

## CROSS-POST OBSERVATIONS

### The LinkedIn Hook Formula

Brian's LinkedIn hooks consistently do one of three things:

1. **Contrarian permission**: "You can do the thing everyone says is hard/wrong. But here's what still matters."
   > "Vibe coding isn't just a buzzword — it's real. And it's about to change your pitch."

2. **Question from the audience's mouth**: Reproduces the question he gets asked constantly.
   > "Founders keep asking me: Do I really need to write my own pitch?"

3. **Specific tension**: Names a contradiction or paradox in the first line.
   > "The best pitches I've seen weren't the most polished. They were the most honest."

**The hook must create one of: tension, curiosity, or permission.** Generic claims don't pull.

---

### LinkedIn Body Rhythm

Brian's LinkedIn body has a distinct rhythm:

- **Never more than 2-3 sentences in a block before a line break**
- **Short sentences follow long ones**: Setup → punch → setup → punch
- **Each paragraph does one thing**: makes one point, asks one question, delivers one contrast
- **Never summarizes what you just said**: Doesn't recap. Moves forward.

Example of correct rhythm:
```
The first 10 seconds of your pitch set the frame for everything that follows.

Investors are listening for one thing: do I believe this person?

Not the market. Not the model. The person.

That's what you're actually pitching.
```

Wrong rhythm (too even, too summarizing):
```
The opening of your pitch is very important. Investors want to believe in you as a founder.
This means that you need to think carefully about how you start. The person matters more than
the deck in many ways.
```

---

### LinkedIn CTA Patterns

Brian almost never ends with "comment below" or "follow me." His closers are:

- **A statement that provokes thought**: "That's not a limitation. That's what pitching actually is."
- **A reframe that redefines the topic**: "Just don't mistake the demo for the pitch."
- **A soft permission**: "So if you're sitting on an idea: yes, build the demo."
- **A one-liner that sounds like advice from a friend**: "When in doubt, cut the slide."

**What he avoids**:
- "Let me know what you think in the comments!"
- "Follow for more founder tips"
- "DM me if you want to chat"
- "Tag a founder who needs to hear this"

---

### LinkedIn Length

- **Target**: 150-250 words
- **Max**: 300 words (rarely)
- **Min**: 100 words (only for high-impact, highly specific takes)

If it requires scrolling more than one phone screen, cut it.

---

### What Makes a Brian LinkedIn Post vs. a Newsletter Section

| LinkedIn Post | Newsletter Section |
|---|---|
| Standalone argument | Part of a larger arc |
| Ends with a thought, not a task | Often ends with a call-to-action (check the link, reply, etc.) |
| No HC Update | Usually includes HC Update |
| No My Founder Story | Often has My Founder Story |
| No sign-off | Always "Stay Humble!" |
| 150-300 words | 800-1,500 words total |
| Hook must work without context | Can assume reader knows Brian |

---

### Converting a Newsletter Section to a LinkedIn Post

When you have a newsletter outline and need a LinkedIn post from it:

1. **Find the sharpest contrast pair** in the outline. That becomes your hook.
2. **Extract the tactical core** — what's the one thing someone should do differently after reading this?
3. **Remove all the scaffolding**: no "In this issue..." no "Let's start with..." no "First, I want to share..."
4. **Write the argument in blocks**, not paragraphs. Short lines. Blank lines between.
5. **End with the reframe**, not the summary.
6. **Cut anything that feels like it's there for completeness**. LinkedIn posts don't need to be complete. They need to be true.
